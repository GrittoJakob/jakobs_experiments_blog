MPO Algorithm: Explanation and Implementation Details

Maximum a Posteriori Policy Optimization (MPO) is an off-policy actor-critic algorithm based on an EM-style policy improvement procedure. In my view, MPO is particularly interesting because it is often more robust to hyperparameter choices than many other off-policy methods, while also using a policy update that does not fit neatly into the usual categories of pure policy-gradient or value-gradient methods. This post summarizes the main ideas behind MPO and highlights practical details from my own implementation.

MPO RL Off-Policy

Motivation

This project originally started as part of a course at the Technical University of Munich. The broader goal was to explore whether sim-to-real transfer could be improved with meta-reinforcement learning. Since many current Meta-RL algorithms suffer from poor sample efficiency, the idea was to combine RL2 with a strong off-policy learner, namely MPO.

In practice, however, this combination did not work as well as expected. A major issue was runtime: making the overall system stable required repeated backpropagation through time, which made the training procedure expensive and difficult to scale. For that reason, I decided to focus on MPO itself. Even independently of the original Meta-RL setting, MPO turned out to be a very interesting algorithm from both a conceptual and implementation perspective.

Setup

Throughout this post, I use the standard notation of a Markov decision process. States are denoted by \(s\), actions by \(a\), rewards by \(r\), and the policy by \( \pi(a \mid s) \). The critic estimates an action-value function \(Q(s,a)\).

MPO follows the general actor-critic structure:

  • Policy evaluation: learn a critic that approximates the action-value function.
  • Policy improvement: update the actor using an EM-style objective.

Core Optimization Idea

The critic can in principle be trained with a range of standard off-policy targets. For example, one may use one-step temporal-difference learning, multi-step targets, or more advanced return estimators such as Retrace. In my own implementation, I used one-step TD learning because it is simple, stable, and does not depend on more involved sequential storage and update logic. Other choices are certainly possible and may work better depending on the environment and training setup.

In my implementation, the critic target is

$$ q_{\text{target}} = r + m \gamma \, \mathbb{E}_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}[Q_{\text{target}}(s', a')], $$

where \(m\) is a bootstrap mask that is zero at terminal transitions and one otherwise. The current critic prediction is

$$ q_{\text{current}} = Q_\phi(s, a), $$

and the critic is trained with a mean squared error objective:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{critic}}(\phi) = \left( q_{\text{target}} - q_{\text{current}} \right)^2. $$

In code, this corresponds to computing a TD target, evaluating the critic at the sampled state-action pairs, and minimizing the MSE between both quantities.

MPO and Policy Improvement

The most interesting part of MPO is the policy improvement step. As already mentioned, MPO is not naturally described as a standard policy-gradient method. Instead, it performs policy improvement through an Expectation-Maximization-style procedure. Conceptually, MPO separates the improvement step into two phases:

  • E-step: construct an auxiliary action distribution \(q(a \mid s)\) that gives higher weight to actions with high critic values.
  • M-step: fit the parameterized policy \( \pi(a \mid s) \) to that auxiliary distribution while enforcing trust-region style constraints.

This decomposition is elegant because it turns policy improvement into a constrained fitting problem rather than a direct gradient step on the actor. That is one reason why MPO often feels numerically well-behaved in practice.

Short Note on the EM Algorithm

Since MPO is motivated by an EM-style update, it is worth briefly recalling the general idea behind the EM algorithm. EM appears in several areas of machine learning, including Gaussian mixture models, latent-variable models, and variational inference. The key idea is to replace a difficult optimization problem by alternating between two simpler steps.

In the classical latent-variable setting, we would like to optimize a log-likelihood whose exact posterior is difficult or expensive to compute. EM introduces an auxiliary distribution and iteratively improves a lower bound on the original objective. The E-step updates the auxiliary distribution, while the M-step updates the model parameters given that distribution.

MPO does not use EM in exactly the same probabilistic modeling sense, but the analogy is very useful: first construct an improved non-parametric target distribution over actions, then project that target back into the parametric policy class.

E-Step

In the E-step, MPO computes an auxiliary distribution over actions that is biased toward high-value actions under the current critic. A common form is

$$ q(a \mid s) \propto \pi(a \mid s)\exp\left(\frac{Q(s,a)}{\eta}\right), $$

where \( \eta \) is a temperature parameter. Intuitively, this expression reweights the current policy by exponentiated Q-values: actions with larger estimated value receive more mass, but the strength of the reweighting is controlled by \( \eta \). If \( \eta \) is large, the reweighting is softer; if \( \eta \) is small, the auxiliary distribution becomes more concentrated on high-value actions.

In practice, this step is usually implemented with sampled candidate actions, because computing the normalized distribution exactly is not feasible in continuous action spaces.

M-Step

In the M-step, the policy is updated to match the auxiliary distribution while keeping the update controlled. The idea is not simply to chase the best actions greedily, but to make a stable update that stays reasonably close to the current policy.

A generic way to describe this step is:

$$ \max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \mathbb{E}_{a \sim q(\cdot \mid s)} \left[ \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) \right] \right] \quad \text{subject to a KL constraint.} $$

The KL constraint acts as a trust region. It prevents the actor from changing too aggressively in a single update and is one of the main ingredients behind the empirical stability of MPO.

Depending on the exact implementation, the KL constraints may be split into separate constraints for the mean and covariance of a Gaussian policy, or handled via dual variables. This is one of the places where implementation details matter a lot.

Why is MPO Interesting?

There are several reasons why MPO stands out as an appealing algorithm:

  • It is off-policy and can therefore use replay data efficiently.
  • Its policy update is constrained, which often improves stability.
  • The EM-style structure gives a clear interpretation of policy improvement as “construct target actions first, then fit the actor.”
  • In practice, it often feels less brittle than several alternative off-policy actor-critic methods.

Of course, this does not mean MPO is always the best choice. Its implementation is somewhat more involved than simpler baselines, and details such as action sampling, dual optimization, and KL handling can make a noticeable difference.

Implementation Notes

The implementation details matter a lot for MPO. In particular, the following components deserve careful attention:

  • How actions are sampled for the E-step.
  • How the critic target is estimated.
  • How the KL constraints are enforced in the M-step.
  • How dual variables such as \( \eta \) are optimized.
  • How numerical instability is handled for exponentiated Q-values.
  • How target networks and update schedules are chosen.

One practical lesson from my experiments is that MPO can indeed be robust, but only once the implementation is internally consistent. Small mismatches between critic scaling, action sampling, and KL handling can easily lead to unstable or misleading behavior.

Empirical Observations

In my own experiments, the most striking observation was that the algorithmic idea of MPO is elegant and compelling, but getting all implementation details right takes time. Once the training loop, target computation, and actor update were aligned properly, the method behaved much more reliably. That said, runtime and engineering complexity were still non-trivial, especially in the broader context of combining MPO with Meta-RL style methods.
