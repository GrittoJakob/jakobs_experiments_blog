MPO Algorithm: Explanation and Implementation Details

Maximum a Posteriori Policy Optimization (MPO) is an off-policy actor-critic algorithm based on an EM-style policy improvement procedure. In my view, MPO is particularly interesting because it is often more robust to hyperparameter choices than many other off-policy methods, while also using a policy update that does not fit neatly into the usual categories of pure policy-gradient or value-gradient methods. In this post, I will summarize the main ideas behind MPO and give some details from my own implementation.

MPO RL Off-Policy

Motivation

This project originally started as part of a course at the Technical University of Munich. The initial goal was to explore whether sim-to-real transfer could be improved with meta-reinforcement learning. Since many current Meta-RL algorithms suffer from poor sample efficiency, the idea was to combine RL2 with a strong off-policy learner, the MPO.

In practice, however, this combination did not work as well as expected. A major issue was the runtime. In order to update our recurrent actor unit we required repeated backpropagation through time, which made the training procedure very expensive and difficult to scale. For that reason, I decided to focus on MPO itself. Even independently of the original Meta-RL setting, MPO turned out to be a very interesting algorithm from both a conceptual and implementation perspective.

Setup

Throughout this post, I use the standard notation of a Markov decision process. States are denoted by \(s\), actions by \(a\), rewards by \(r\), and the policy by \( \pi(a \mid s) \). The critic estimates an action-value function \(Q(s,a)\). Ok, then let' s start:)

MPO follows the general actor-critic structure:

  • Policy evaluation: learn a critic that approximates the action-value function.
  • Policy improvement: update the actor using an EM-style objective.

Policy Evaluation

The critic can in principle be trained with a range of standard off-policy targets. For example, one may use one-step temporal-difference learning, multi-step targets, or more advanced return estimators such as Retrace. While Deepmind suggests using the Retrace algorithm, in my implementation I used one-step TD learning because it is simple, stable, and does not depend on more sequential storage and update logic. However, Deepmind suggests in their paper using the Retrace algorithm.

In my implementation, the critic target is

$$ Q_{\text{target}} = r + m \gamma \, \mathbb{E}_{a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')}[Q_{\text{target}}(s', a')], $$

where \(m\) is a bootstrap mask that is zero at terminal transitions and one otherwise. The current critic prediction is

$$ Q_{\text{current}} = Q_\phi(s, a), $$

and the critic is trained with a mean squared error objective:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{critic}}(\phi) = \left( q_{\text{target}} - q_{\text{current}} \right)^2. $$

MPO and Policy Improvement

The most interesting part of MPO is the policy improvement step. As already mentioned, MPO can not easily be categorized as a standard policy-gradient method. Instead, it performs policy improvement through an Expectation-Maximization-style procedure. Conceptually, MPO separates the improvement step into two phases:

  • E-step: construct an auxiliary (non-parametric) action distribution \(q(a \mid s)\) that gives higher weight to actions with high critic values.
  • M-step: fit the parameterized policy \( \pi(a \mid s) \) to that auxiliary distribution

Short Note on the EM Algorithm

Since MPO is motivated by an EM-style update, it is worth to briefly recall the general idea behind the EM algorithm. EM appears in several areas of machine learning, including Gaussian mixture models, latent-variable models, and variational inference. The key idea is to replace a difficult optimization problem by alternating between two simpler steps.

In the classical latent-variable setting, we would like to optimize a log-likelihood whose exact posterior is difficult to compute. EM introduces an auxiliary distribution and iteratively improves a lower bound on the original objective. The E-step updates the auxiliary distribution, while the M-step updates the model parameters given that distribution.

MPO does not use EM in exactly the same probabilistic way, but the analogy is very useful: first construct an improved non-parametric target distribution over actions, then project that target back into the parametric policy class.

E-Step

In the E-step, MPO computes an auxiliary distribution over actions by reweighting the current policy with critic values. The goal is to improve the action distribution while keeping it close to the current policy \( \pi_i(a \mid s) \). In the non-parametric variant of MPO, this is done by solving a constrained optimization problem: maximize the expected Q-value under \( q(a \mid s) \), subject to a KL constraint and the normalization of \( q \). :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

More precisely, the constrained E-step can be written as

$$ \begin{aligned} \max_q \quad & \int \mu(s)\int q(a \mid s)\,Q^{\pi_i}(s,a)\,da\,ds \\ \text{subject to} \quad & \int \mu(s)\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(q(a \mid s)\,\|\,\pi_i(a \mid s)\right)\,ds < \epsilon, \\ & \int q(a \mid s)\,da = 1 \quad \text{for all } s. \end{aligned} $$

Here, \( \mu(s) \) denotes the state distribution used in the update, which in practice is usually approximated by replay-buffer samples. The KL constraint controls how far the improved distribution is allowed to move away from the current policy. This trust-region style constraint is one of the main reasons why MPO tends to behave robustly in practice. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

To solve this problem, we form the Lagrangian

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}(q,\eta,\gamma) =\;& \int \mu(s)\int q(a \mid s)\,Q^{\pi_i}(s,a)\,da\,ds \\ &+ \eta\left( \epsilon - \int \mu(s)\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(q(a \mid s)\,\|\,\pi_i(a \mid s)\right)\,ds \right) \\ &+ \int \mu(s)\,\gamma(s) \left( 1 - \int q(a \mid s)\,da \right)\,ds , \end{aligned} $$

where \( \eta \ge 0 \) is the Lagrange multiplier for the KL constraint and \( \gamma(s) \) enforces that \( q(a \mid s) \) integrates to one for each state. Taking the derivative with respect to \( q(a \mid s) \), setting it to zero, and rearranging terms yields the closed-form optimizer. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

The resulting optimal auxiliary distribution is given by

$$ q_i(a \mid s) = \frac{ \pi_i(a \mid s)\, \exp\!\left(\frac{Q^{\pi_i}(s,a)}{\eta}\right) }{ \int \pi_i(a' \mid s)\, \exp\!\left(\frac{Q^{\pi_i}(s,a')}{\eta}\right)\,da' }. $$

While this expression is derived in continuous form, it is generally not tractable in practice due to the integral in the denominator. Therefore, MPO implementations approximate this distribution using a finite set of sampled actions.

In practice, we sample \(N\) actions \(a_i \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)\) and compute normalized weights using a softmax over Q-values:

$$ q_{ij} = q(a_i, s_j) = \frac{ \exp\!\left(\frac{Q(s_j, a_i)}{\eta}\right) }{ \sum_{l=1}^{N} \exp\!\left(\frac{Q(s_j, a_l)}{\eta}\right) }. $$

This corresponds to a softmax over sampled Q-values and is the form typically used in practical implementations of MPO. Intuitively, this assigns higher probability mass to actions with larger Q-values while still maintaining a normalized distribution.

The temperature parameter \( \eta \) is obtained by minimizing the dual function

$$ g(\eta) = \eta \epsilon + \eta \sum_j \log \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \exp\!\left(\frac{Q(s_j,a_i)}{\eta}\right) \right), $$

which is a convex function and can be optimized efficiently. This formulation is consistent with the practical implementation described in follow-up work and reflects how MPO is typically used in modern deep RL codebases.

M-Step

In the M-step, the policy is updated to match the auxiliary distribution while keeping the update controlled. The idea is not simply to chase the best actions greedily, but to make a stable update that stays reasonably close to the current policy.

A generic way to describe this step is:

$$ \max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \mathbb{E}_{a \sim q(\cdot \mid s)} \left[ \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) \right] \right] \quad \text{subject to a KL constraint.} $$

The KL constraint acts as a trust region. It prevents the actor from changing too aggressively in a single update and is one of the main ingredients behind the empirical stability of MPO.

Depending on the exact implementation, the KL constraints may be split into separate constraints for the mean and covariance of a Gaussian policy, or handled via dual variables. This is one of the places where implementation details matter a lot.

Why is MPO Interesting?

There are several reasons why MPO stands out as an appealing algorithm:

  • It is off-policy and can therefore use replay data efficiently.
  • Its policy update is constrained, which often improves stability.
  • The EM-style structure gives a clear interpretation of policy improvement as “construct target actions first, then fit the actor.”
  • In practice, it often feels less brittle than several alternative off-policy actor-critic methods.

Of course, this does not mean MPO is always the best choice. Its implementation is somewhat more involved than simpler baselines, and details such as action sampling, dual optimization, and KL handling can make a noticeable difference.

Implementation Notes

The implementation details matter a lot for MPO. In particular, the following components deserve careful attention:

  • How actions are sampled for the E-step.
  • How the critic target is estimated.
  • How the KL constraints are enforced in the M-step.
  • How dual variables such as \( \eta \) are optimized.
  • How numerical instability is handled for exponentiated Q-values.
  • How target networks and update schedules are chosen.

One practical lesson from my experiments is that MPO can indeed be robust, but only once the implementation is internally consistent. Small mismatches between critic scaling, action sampling, and KL handling can easily lead to unstable or misleading behavior.

Empirical Observations

In my own experiments, the most striking observation was that the algorithmic idea of MPO is elegant and compelling, but getting all implementation details right takes time. Once the training loop, target computation, and actor update were aligned properly, the method behaved much more reliably. That said, runtime and engineering complexity were still non-trivial, especially in the broader context of combining MPO with Meta-RL style methods.
